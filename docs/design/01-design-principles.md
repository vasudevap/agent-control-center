# Atlas Design Principles

**Status:** Approved  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Owner:** Product Design

---

# Purpose

This document defines the enduring design principles that govern every Atlas experience.

These principles are the design constitution of the product. Every workflow, screen, component, and interaction should align with them.

---

# Core Principles

## 1. Control Before Automation

Atlas exists to help operators control autonomous systems.

Automation must always remain subordinate to human intent.

---

## 2. Trust Through Evidence

Trust is earned through observable evidence.

Every significant action should be explainable, inspectable, and auditable.

---

## 3. Operational Clarity

Interfaces should answer operational questions before exposing configuration.

Users should immediately understand:

- current state
- required action
- operational risk
- system health

---

## 4. Progressive Disclosure

Present the minimum information required for the current task while allowing deeper inspection without losing context.

---

## 5. Consistency Over Novelty

Equivalent concepts behave identically throughout Atlas.

Consistency improves learnability, predictability, and trust.

---

## 6. Calm Complexity

Atlas should simplify complexity through structure, not by hiding important information.

---

## 7. Human Authority

Operators can always inspect, pause, stop, override, or approve autonomous behavior where appropriate.

---

## 8. Observable by Default

Every meaningful action leaves evidence.

Nothing important should occur silently.

---

## 9. Safe by Default

High-risk operations require deliberate interaction.

The cost of an action should reflect its potential impact.

---

## 10. Enterprise Quality

Atlas favors precision, restraint, accessibility, and reliability over visual novelty.

---

# Interaction Principles

- Information before configuration.
- Objects before actions.
- State before settings.
- Primary actions remain visible.
- Destructive actions are explicit.
- Recovery paths are always available.
- Feedback is immediate and meaningful.

---

# Information Principles

Every primary screen answers one operational question.

Hierarchy should prioritize:

1. Identity
2. State
3. Risk
4. Action
5. Activity
6. History
7. Detail

---

# Visual Principles

The interface should be:

- restrained
- precise
- information-rich
- accessible
- professional
- desktop-first

Visual design communicates meaning rather than decoration.

---

# Motion Principles

Motion should communicate:

- transition
- hierarchy
- progress
- causality

Avoid decorative animation.

---

# Content Principles

Atlas communicates like an experienced technical operator.

Content should be:

- concise
- factual
- actionable
- consistent
- calm

Avoid hype, anthropomorphism, and marketing language in the product UI.

---

# Accessibility Principles

Atlas targets WCAG 2.2 AA.

Accessibility is a core design requirement and applies to every component and workflow.

---

# Decision Test

Before approving any design, confirm:

1. Does it increase operational clarity?
2. Does it preserve user control?
3. Does it improve trust?
4. Is the current state obvious?
5. Is required action obvious?
6. Is behavior consistent?
7. Does it scale to enterprise use?
8. Is it accessible?
9. Can it be implemented consistently?
10. Would this still be appropriate in five years?

If any answer is no, revise the design.

---

# Success Criteria

Atlas should consistently feel:

- operational
- trustworthy
- governed
- predictable
- modern
- enterprise-ready

The best interface is one that enables confident operation of autonomous systems while remaining calm, understandable, and reliable.
